SNEAKERS
canvas to express ideological warfare

Since 19th century, sneakers has been about much more than athletics. Sneakers signified everything from national identity, race and class to masculinity and criminality. They are magnets for social and political meaning, intended or otherwise.
Not until the 1920s that industrialisation made sneakers widely available and affordable. Politics also fueled the rise of sneakers as much as athletics. WWI increased interest in physical culture, which became linked to nationalism. Countries encouraged their citizens to exercise not just for physical perfection, but to prepare for upcoming war. The sneakers became one of the most democratised forms of footwear at the height of fascism. [1]

In the beginning of the 90’s sneakers were for a niche subcultures, mostly skaters, surfers, basketball players and collectors, but in the late 90’s and the beginning of the 2000 sneakers became popular in much larger and more diverse group of consumers. This shift can be attributed to a few factors in the retail sector, including the increased focus on direct-to-consumer sales at major sportswear brands like Nike and Adidas, and the rise of influencers on social media. [2]
Ulta popular sneakers are being designed in collaboration with different celebrities and designers. There are people buying the shoes without knowing anything about the celebrity or the designer, and there are people who buy them only because of them.

Collaborations are a frequent occurrence in the broader retail industry, of course, but they are constantly appearing in the sneaker world, when brands work with celebrities, influencers and even other brands to create hype and reach new consumers.

The branding field has become a setting for one of the most passionate and controversial ways of protesting for social justice; It has increasingly become a space for cultural and political debates; and the CEO’s of brands have taken public stands on everything from immigration to criminal justice.
The line designed, by brazilian architect Oscar Niemeyer, for Converse in 2013, contained hidden human right slogans and symbols. [3] But what about the dozens of factory workers, making Converse sneakers in Indonesia, who are being routinely abused on their job? Another example is the white supremacist website, The Daily Stormer, which published an article where it praised New Balance for being the “official brand of the Trump Revolution.” It happened after the VP of New Balance, Matt LeBretton, said that “things are going to move in the right direction” with Trump as president. After that some enraged customers took to social media to burn their shoes to protest against the brand.[4]

Inevitably, some of the statements made through sneakers were accused of going too far. This is one of the problems that can arise in sneakers industries. The intent, the message, and the realities of production don’t always line up.
